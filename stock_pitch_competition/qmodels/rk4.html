The System of Equations
Equation 1 — Bitcoin Price
dB/dt = αB · (1 - S) · (1/supply_remaining) · B
Bitcoin's rate of change is driven by its own momentum (α), dampened by macro risk sentiment (S), and amplified by supply scarcity. As supply_remaining → 0 (approaching the 21M cap), the scarcity multiplier increases. Key insight: This equation has no Coinbase term. Bitcoin's growth function is independent of any exchange.
Equation 2 — Transaction Volume
dV/dt = βV · (dB/dt / B) · V · (1 - market_share_loss)
Volume tracks Bitcoin's percentage rate of change — not its absolute price. When Bitcoin momentum slows, volume decays even if price is high. market_share_loss represents Binance, Kraken, DEXs, ATMs, PayPal — the 17-year reality that Bitcoin trades everywhere, not just Coinbase. This term grows over time, compressing Coinbase's slice of the pie.
Equation 3 — Macro Risk Sentiment
dS/dt = γS · (external_shock) - δS · S
Sentiment spikes on external shocks (tariffs, AI unemployment, geopolitical events) and decays naturally over time. Critical: When S rises, it simultaneously hits B(t) and V(t) — a double compression on Coinbase revenue, which then hits C(t) with a third compounding effect. This is the cascade your thesis describes.
Equation 4 — Coinbase Share Price
dC/dt = λC · (V/V₀) · (B/B₀) - μC · S · C - νC · (leverage_ratio) · C
COIN rises with normalized volume and Bitcoin price. It falls with sentiment shocks and over-leverage. The thesis is in νC: the leverage term. When investors get excited about price swings (your "delta" definition of over-leverage), the unwind is violent. This is why COIN dropped ~50% from Q4 2025 — not regulatory clarity, but leverage flush meeting sentiment shock.
The RK4 Method — Step By Step
WHY RK4?
A simple model would assume Bitcoin grows at a constant rate — that's Euler's method, and it's wrong because the rate itself changes. RK4 takes four slope estimates per time step and weights them together. This captures the nonlinearity your thesis describes — the cycle accelerates, decelerates, and reverses. It's an initial value problem: we know where we are today (B₀, V₀, S₀, C₀) and we want to know where the system goes.
01
K₁ — Slope at the Start
k₁_B = f_B(t, B, V, S)
k₁_V = f_V(t, B, V, S)
k₁_S = f_S(t, S)
k₁_C = f_C(t, B, V, S, C)
Evaluate all four differential equations at the current moment using today's known values. This is your baseline — where the system is heading right now. Think of it as: if nothing changes, what's the trajectory?
02
K₂ — Slope at the Midpoint (using K₁)
k₂_B = f_B(t+h/2, B + h/2·k₁_B, ...)
k₂_V = f_V(t+h/2, ...+ h/2·k₁_V)
k₂_S = f_S(t+h/2, S + h/2·k₁_S)
k₂_C = f_C(t+h/2, ...+ h/2·k₁_C)
Jump halfway through the time step using K₁'s slope, then re-evaluate. This corrects for the fact that Bitcoin doesn't travel in a straight line — it curves. The midpoint gives a better estimate of where the arc is heading.
03
K₃ — Slope at the Midpoint (using K₂)
k₃_B = f_B(t+h/2, B + h/2·k₂_B, ...)
k₃_V = f_V(t+h/2, ...+ h/2·k₂_V)
k₃_S = f_S(t+h/2, S + h/2·k₂_S)
k₃_C = f_C(t+h/2, ...+ h/2·k₂_C)
Same midpoint, but now using K₂'s refined estimate. This is RK4's key advantage — it samples the midpoint twice with improving accuracy. This is where the cycle curvature gets properly captured.
04
K₄ — Slope at the End
k₄_B = f_B(t+h, B + h·k₃_B, ...)
k₄_V = f_V(t+h, ...+ h·k₃_V)
k₄_S = f_S(t+h, S + h·k₃_S)
k₄_C = f_C(t+h, ...+ h·k₃_C)
Now jump to the full end of the time step using K₃'s slope. This anchors the far end of the arc — critical for capturing the leverage unwind dynamic where the end of a cycle looks nothing like the start.
FINAL UPDATE — Weighted Average of All Four Slopes
C(t+h) = C(t) + (h/6) · (k₁_C + 2k₂_C + 2k₃_C + k₄_C)
The middle slopes (K₂, K₃) are weighted double because the midpoint carries more information about the curve than the endpoints. This is the weighted average that makes RK4 accurate to the 4th order — error shrinks by h⁴ each step.

Applied to your thesis: Each time step h represents one quarter. You run this forward 40 steps (10 years). The output C(t) at each step shows where Coinbase's price goes under different assumptions about Bitcoin's cycle, market share erosion, and sentiment shocks. The divergence between C(t) and B(t) — normalized — is the proof. If Coinbase's growth truly tracked Bitcoin's organic demand, the ratio should stay constant. It won't.

Appendix — Variable Definitions
S(t) — Macro Risk Sentiment
External, time-varying shock input
Macro risk sentiment is defined as the aggregate of external conditions that suppress investor risk appetite independently of cryptocurrency fundamentals. It encompasses three non-overlapping categories:

Geopolitical: Trade policy changes, tariffs, sanctions, military conflict, and sovereign debt stress. These events compress risk appetite broadly across equities and digital assets simultaneously — reflected in Bitcoin's 0.55 correlation with the S&P 500 as of Q1 2026.

Technological displacement: AI-driven unemployment concerns, cybersecurity threats to financial infrastructure, and systemic platform risk. These are distinct from geopolitical events in that they affect the labor and productivity assumptions underlying equity valuations, including Coinbase's growth narrative.

Monetary conditions: Federal Reserve policy direction, real interest rates, and dollar strength. These set the opportunity cost of holding non-yielding assets like Bitcoin and directly compress or expand the risk premium investors assign to speculative equities like COIN.

Critically, S(t) is an input to the system, not a function of transaction volume. Transaction volume (V) is a separate downstream variable that responds to S(t) — they are not interchangeable.
V(t) — Transaction Flows
Coinbase revenue mechanism — derivative of Bitcoin momentum
Transaction flows are defined as the measurable movement of capital through Coinbase's exchange infrastructure across four distinct channels:

Institutional equity flows: Net buying or selling of COIN shares by institutional participants, including ETF rebalancing, hedge fund positioning, and insider activity. These reflect forward-looking sentiment on Coinbase's business model, not current cryptocurrency demand.

On-chain asset flows: Net inflows and outflows of BTC, ETH, SOL, and USDC across Coinbase's custodial and trading infrastructure. This is the primary revenue driver — exchange transaction fees scale directly with this volume.

Stablecoin circulation: USDC mint and redemption activity through Circle's partnership with Coinbase. This component is partially insulated from Bitcoin's cycle but remains correlated with broader crypto market participation.

Total addressable market compression: The structural erosion of Coinbase's share of global crypto transaction volume as capital routes through Binance, Kraken, OKX, DEXs, ATMs, PayPal, and peer-to-peer channels. This is the market share loss term in the model and is the most consequential long-run variable — Bitcoin has traded across all of these venues for 17 years without Coinbase's involvement.
Over-Leverage — The Delta Term (ν)
Correlation amplifier between B(t) and C(t)
Over-leverage is defined as the rate of change in correlation between Bitcoin price and Coinbase share price during periods of elevated market excitement — not as a static measure of debt or margin usage.

In practice, when Bitcoin begins a rapid upward move, leveraged derivatives positions accumulate in both BTC perpetual futures and COIN equity options. This temporarily tightens the B(t)→C(t) relationship beyond its structural baseline. The leverage term ν captures this dynamic as a multiplier on sentiment-driven drawdowns — when S(t) spikes and the leverage unwind begins, COIN's decline is amplified relative to Bitcoin's because COIN carries both the asset class risk and the operational equity risk simultaneously.

This is why COIN declined approximately 50% from its Q4 2025 peak while Bitcoin's drawdown was comparatively contained. The leverage flush and the sentiment shock compounded. Subscription and service revenue — now approximately 40% of non-GAAP financials — provided partial insulation, but the dominant transaction revenue exposure ensured the cascade followed the model's predicted path.
A NOTE ON VARIABLE INDEPENDENCE
The three input variables — S(t), V(t), and the leverage ratio ν — are defined to be structurally non-overlapping in the model even though they correlate empirically. Sentiment S(t) measures external conditions arriving from outside the crypto system. Volume V(t) measures capital movement within Coinbase's infrastructure responding to those conditions. Leverage ν measures the amplification coefficient on the relationship between the underlying asset and the equity during the response. Each occupies a distinct causal position in the chain: shock → flow → amplification → price outcome. Conflating any two of these would misattribute the source of price movement — which is precisely the error the broader market makes when it credits regulatory clarity for price action that is structurally driven by this three-stage cascade.
