I believe to effectively protect a computer system (hardware, software, network, and data) from attacks, yes, people who would like to protect systems can apply counterattack strategies, but to effectively secure the longevity of their valuables, they can also develop computer systems that are immune to attacks by design. 

For example, the Peer-To-Peer Electronic Cash System written by Satoshi Nakamoto on October 31, 2008, describes a network where nodes can essentially transfer data discreetly, free from attacks or non-anonymous transactions.
This exchange of data takes place on a blockchain where new transactions are broadcast to all nodes, each node collects new transactions into a block, and each node works on finding a difficult proof of work for its block.


Next, when a node finds a proof-of-work, it broadcasts the block to all nodes. Nodes accept the block only if all transactions in it are valid and not already spent. Finally, nodes express their acceptance of the block by working on creating the next block in the chain, using the hash of the accepted block as the previous hash. To improve privacy, nodes do not disclose potentially identifiable information.


It is important to note that "to modify a past block, an attacker would have to redo the proof-of-work of the block and all blocks after it and then catch up with and surpass the work of the honest nodes" (Nakamoto, 2008, p. 8). Indeed, the methodology explained by Satoshi presents a strenuous task for the attacker: the Gambler's Ruin Problem, where the probability of the attacker catching up to a node decreases exponentially. However, how viable is this approach in keeping the system immune to attacks?


The price of one bitcoin was $1.00 on February 9, 2011, and reached a price of $97,000+ on January 14, 2026. Many investors opine a myriad of reasons as to why and debate over X, formerly known as Twitter; but we are much more interested in examining the underlying infrastructure.


Essentially, Satoshi built a successful software system based on cryptographic proof instead of trust, allowing any two willing parties to transact directly with each other without the need for a trusted third party. His original work completely changed Web 2.0, then Web 3.0, and defined decentralized finance. However, despite his innovative work, was he able to secure his computer system throughout the entire circulation of the digital asset?


Not long after October 31, 2008, millions of cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin would be reportedly stolen or mismanaged in cases of fraud and laundering throughout its respective market tenure. Currently, attackers employ various scamming techniques to ascertain data about a victim. Some examples include phishing, SIM swaps, and misspelled URLs, "often with a slight deviation from the actual financial institutions' website" (Federal Bureau of Investigation [FBI], n.d.). The cases have been exacerbated by artificial intelligence, where artificially generated false realities in the form of fake videos or voices position attackers as a continuous threat to the safety of a computer system. Attackers access private information like passwords to wallet ledgers and financial accounts using this new technology. They also utilize long-term strategies for maximum exploitation.


Samuel Bankman-Fried was sentenced to 25 years for his orchestration of multiple fraudulent schemes on Thursday, March 28, 2024. He was the founder of the cryptocurrency exchange FTX and the cryptocurrency trading firm Alameda Research.

According to the U.S. Attorney Damian Williams, "Samuel Bankman-Fried orchestrated one of the largest financial frauds in history, stealing over $8 billion of his customers' money" (U.S. Department of Justice, 2024, para. 1). Basically, Samuel told his customers that deposits were kept safe in custody, but used surreptitious channels to make investments for his own benefit. Attackers have found ways to get around the strenuous effort of Satoshi's original method to cause havoc.


Moreover, quantum computers capable of conducting Shor's algorithm, which can recover private keys from public keys, pose an incoming threat. This renders the "structured number-theoretic problems, integer factorization, and discrete logarithm problems that underpin the digital signature and key agreement schemes used throughout blockchain systems" (Deegen et al., 2026, p. 1) obsolete.


Various other efforts are being exerted by developers and companies like Coinbase Global, Incorporated to protect systems like wallet ledgers from attackers, because once attackers are in, they are notorious for instantly draining accounts. The Module-Lattice-Based Digital Signature Algorithm (ML-DSA) was introduced on August 13, 2024, by the National Institute of Standards and Technology in an effort to generate and verify digital signatures even in the presence of attackers with large-scale quantum computers.


Currently, hierarchical deterministic (HD) wallets are not secure because they rely on elliptic-curve schemes, which are linear algebraic structures. A Lattice HD Wallet—the Raccoon-G HD Wallet—has recently been created by Deegen et al. to hash a seed which produces two 32-byte outputs: a master keypair and a master chain code.


ML-DSA supports only hardened derivation, while this new wallet implements non-hardened derivation, whereby keys are derived deterministically from the single master key. This project was developed by Project Eleven, an online programming group focused on sharing the importance of quantum resistance in Bitcoin. 

Why is this important? Most cybersecurity responses are reactive. A breach happens then a patch follows. What Deegen et al. are doing is proactive redesign at the architectural level. They are ensuring a generational strong foundation before any branches. There is an emphasis being placed on retaining the purity of the electronic peer-to-peer cash system. Overall, true security is an end-to-end model owned by the programmers and not left to be susceptible at any given entry or exit point.


Ultimately, Satoshi attempted to eliminate the need for human trust entirely by replacing it with mathematical certainty. The cryptography was held. What failed was the human layer surrounding it—the exchanges, the custodians, and the bad actors who exploited the trust that the system was specifically designed to remove.


In conclusion, all of these examples illustrate that no matter the efforts to prevent attacks, the threat to computer systems is far from over—especially if you have something valuable that needs to be protected. How to build a computer system that is immune to attacks is an extremely difficult question for programmers to answer. It is also an important question for cybersecurity professionals to answer. In the case of Bitcoin, it requires patience, an analysis of potential edge cases, and constant iteration through communication of ideas.

Developers can develop firewalls or computer systems that are immune to attacks, like Satoshi did, but may face an ethical dilemma to strive for security by design–especially if they have an immediate impact on the well-being of millions (e.g., health applications, cloud databases, or electronic voting ballots). Decreasing the probability of attacks in computer systems calls for ensuring safety by design, not just through firewalls.
